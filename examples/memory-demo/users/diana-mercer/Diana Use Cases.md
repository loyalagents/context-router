# Diana Use Cases

## Travel Booking

- **JetBlue sells out, Delta needs a seat map decision.** Diana asks Claude to book BOS-SFO for a Google Cloud Next trip. JetBlue has no aisle seats remaining in her fare class. Claude checks Delta, finds an aisle on a connection through JFK vs. a middle seat on a direct. Her preference store has `checkedBags: false` and no explicit policy on connections - Claude flags the tradeoff and asks before booking rather than choosing silently.
- **Conference hotel block is full.** The CloudNativeCon room block expires. Claude searches Hotels.com, filters to Marriott Bonvoy properties within walkable distance of the Moscone Center, and surfaces three options ranked by loyalty tier and nightly rate - without Diana having to re-enter her loyalty program or walking-distance requirement.
- **Reimbursement form at a new employer requires loyalty numbers she hasn't loaded.** Crestline's travel tool asks for Delta SkyMiles and Marriott Bonvoy numbers at expense submission time. Her preference store has the fields but they're null. Claude flags which specific fields are missing and drafts a reminder to fill them rather than silently submitting blanks.
- **Red-eye vs. early morning disambiguation.** Diana is booking BOS-LAX for a Monday 9am meeting. Claude knows she prefers no checked bags but has no sleep/schedule preferences on file. It identifies two viable options - a Sunday red-eye or a very early Monday direct - and asks one targeted question rather than presenting all eleven available flights.

## Conference Registration

- **Single catering free-text field for multiple constraints.** A conference registration form has one 255-character catering notes field. Diana is pescatarian and gluten-sensitive with different severity levels for each. Claude composes a single coherent sentence that prioritizes the gluten nuance ("sensitivity, not celiac - cross-contamination is a caution") without burying it behind the meal preference label, and checks character count before submitting.
- **Session selector with 60+ options and no topic filter.** KubeCon's session picker has no checkbox for "platform engineering." Claude maps her stored topic list against session titles and abstracts, scores each for practitioner vs. analyst speaker ratio, and returns a ranked shortlist of 8 - surfacing two she wouldn't have found by keyword search alone.
- **Badge name vs. legal name conflict.** A new conference platform requires legal name for badge printing but sends all pre-event emails to a "preferred name" field. Her legal name is Diana but a legacy Stratosphere Solutions record in the partner system has "D. Mercer." Claude catches the mismatch before submission and flags the stale record rather than letting it propagate.
- **Speaker bio auto-draft for a CFP.** Diana is submitting a talk proposal to KubeCon on FinOps tooling. Claude pulls her current title, company, and conference topic list from the preference store to draft a 100-word speaker bio and session abstract - she edits tone and content rather than starting from scratch or hunting for a prior bio.

## Personal Shopping

- **Yoga mat replacement with no preferred retailer.** Diana's mat is worn out. She asks Claude to find a replacement. It researches options across multiple retailers using Perplexity for review aggregation, filters by material (she prefers natural rubber based on past purchases if that's logged, or asks once and stores it), and recommends three options with return policy and delivery speed surfaced - she picks, Claude places the order.
- **Spring seed order with delivery timing sensitivity.** Diana wants to order tomato and herb starts for her garden in early May. Claude knows she gardens but needs to know her hardiness zone and last-frost date for the Boston area to recommend appropriate timing and variety. It asks for those two facts, stores them, and selects a vendor that ships live plants with an arrival window after last frost - not just the cheapest option.
- **Gifting a colleague with no personal data shared.** Diana wants to send a thank-you gift to Keiko Tanaka. She doesn't want to share Keiko's preferences with any shopping service. Claude handles the research entirely on Diana's behalf using only publicly available product data, asks Diana one question about budget, and surfaces three options with gift-wrapping and delivery confirmation - Keiko's name never enters a third-party system.

## Dietary / Food in Context

- **Restaurant lookup before a customer dinner in San Francisco.** Diana is hosting a customer dinner and wants a restaurant that works for a pescatarian (herself), an unknown dietary profile for the customer, and a group of four. Claude searches for Japanese or Mediterranean options in a specific neighborhood, filters for gluten-aware kitchens, and drafts a short options summary she can forward to the customer to pick - framing it as "a few options I had in mind" without exposing her dietary constraints unless she chooses to.
- **Hotel room service order at midnight after a flight.** Diana lands late at SFO, checks into a Marriott, and asks Claude to find something to eat from the hotel menu. It knows she's pescatarian, gluten-sensitive, prefers lighter food, and it's late - it filters the room service PDF (if available) or the hotel app menu and surfaces two or three appropriate options without her having to explain her preferences again.

## Cross-Service Portability / Consent

- **New event platform requests full profile access.** A conference partner system Diana hasn't used before prompts her to "connect your profile" via OAuth. Rather than granting full access, her context server generates a scoped token with only the fields the platform actually needs - badge name, email, meal preference, dietary note, and session topics - and presents Diana with a one-screen consent review before sharing.
- **Switching from Claude.ai to a new AI tool for a single task.** A colleague recommends a specialized travel agent tool for a complex multi-city itinerary. Diana doesn't want to re-enter her travel preferences or share her food and hobby data with the new tool. Her preference store issues a travel-scoped export (airlines, seat, bags, trusted traveler, hotel loyalty, typical routes) that the new tool consumes - her gardening habits and yoga schedule never leave her store.
- **Preference drift detection.** Diana switches from JetBlue to Delta as her primary airline after a string of JetBlue delays. She books three consecutive Delta flights via Claude without updating her stored preference. After the third, Claude notes the pattern and asks: *"I've noticed you've chosen Delta over JetBlue for your last three bookings - would you like me to update your preference?"* rather than silently continuing to offer JetBlue first.
